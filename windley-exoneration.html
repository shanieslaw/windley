A Timeline of Injustice

April 1
2005
The Robbery

Two men follow 70-year-old Gerald Ross into his Crown Heights apartment building, robbing him in the elevator of $485 cash and two unsigned money orders. The same morning, Kenneth Windley — who was buying a stove for his mother — purchased one of those money orders from two neighborhood acquaintances at a discount, presenting his real name and driver's license at the appliance store. He had no idea it was stolen.

May–June
2005
Investigation & Misidentification

Police trace the money order to Windley through the appliance store receipt. Ross is shown a photo array six weeks after the robbery and identifies Windley. Three weeks later, he identifies Windley again in a live lineup. Windley is arrested. From the very beginning, he tells investigators the truth: two men sold him the money order. He even provides their nicknames and partial names — information investigators do not pursue.

March
2007
Wrongful Conviction

After a trial at which Windley testifies consistently with what he told police, a Brooklyn jury convicts him of second-degree robbery. Because of prior felony convictions, he is sentenced to 20 years to life. His appeals fail. The Appellate Division, Second Department affirms the conviction in 2010; the Court of Appeals denies leave. A federal habeas petition is also denied.

2010–
2020
Years Behind Bars — and a Man Who Never Stopped Fighting

From prison, Windley continues identifying the two men who robbed Gerald Ross. His fiancée Donna Carter and a private investigator locate Suspect 1 after his parole in 2018. A second investigator tracks down Suspect 2. Both men, by now serving time for a pattern of robberies targeting elderly men in Brooklyn with the same modus operandi as the Ross robbery, agree to come forward. They confirm, in sworn statements and recorded interviews, that Windley had no involvement in the crime.

2023–
2025
Shanies Law Engages — CRU Review

Shanies Law takes on Windley's representation and submits the case to the Kings County District Attorney's Conviction Review Unit, presenting the sworn confessions and corroborating evidence. The CRU launches a thorough reinvestigation, interviewing the actual perpetrators, reviewing prison phone records and emails, and reexamining every piece of trial evidence. The CRU confirms the suspects' identities and finds their accounts plausible and compelling.

March 16–17
2026
Exoneration

Brooklyn DA Eric Gonzalez moves to vacate the conviction. Brooklyn Supreme Court Justice Matthew D'Emic grants the joint application of prosecutors and defense counsel, dismisses the indictment entirely, and Kenneth Windley walks out of the courthouse a free man — accompanied by his mother, Francina Windley Patterson, and his fiancée Donna Carter. DA Gonzalez shakes his hand outside and privately apologizes: "Had we known what the evidence was, this case should have never happened."

02

What Went Wrong

The Windley case is a convergence of well-documented failure modes in the criminal justice system — each individually dangerous, together catastrophic. The Kings County DA's own CRU report acknowledged this was "a cautionary tale of how things can seem one way but, without careful analysis, not be what it purports to be."

👁

Eyewitness Misidentification

Gerald Ross identified Windley from a photo array six weeks after the robbery, and then again in a live lineup three weeks later. Ross acknowledged the man who went through his pockets had been actively hiding his face during the robbery. The description he gave — height, build, complexion — did not clearly match Windley and was inconsistent across accounts. Delayed eyewitness identification under these conditions is a well-established driver of wrongful convictions.

🔍

Investigative Tunnel Vision

From the moment police traced the money order to Windley, the investigation treated him as the answer rather than a lead. Windley told police before his arrest — and testified at trial — that he bought the money order from two men, providing their nicknames and partial names. Investigators did not meaningfully pursue those leads. The two actual perpetrators were still committing the same type of robbery in the same Brooklyn neighborhood for months after the crime.

⚖️

Sentencing Enhancement

Windley had prior felony convictions. This did not mean he committed this robbery — but it ensured that when the jury returned a guilty verdict, the consequence was severe: 20 years to life. New York's persistent felony offender laws meant a marginal case yielded a catastrophic sentence, and the same prior record made courts at every appellate level less receptive to his claims. His record was used to define him rather than examine the evidence.

🔗

Circumstantial Evidence Mistaken for Proof

The prosecution's case rested on a single chain of custody: a stolen money order traced to Windley's purchase. The connection was real. His guilt was not. Windley had provided his real name, ID, and home address at the appliance store — not the behavior of someone concealing criminal activity. The CRU ultimately concluded that the jury, had it known the identities and robbery records of the actual perpetrators, would likely have acquitted.

🚫

Appellate System Failures

Windley's direct appeal to the Second Department failed. Leave to the Court of Appeals was denied. Federal habeas was denied. Every available avenue of post-conviction review rejected him — because each was constrained by the record at trial, a record that didn't include the evidence that would later exonerate him. The courts could only see what the investigation had found. They could not see what it had missed.

📅

No Path to Accountability

The two men who actually robbed Gerald Ross cannot be prosecuted for that crime. The statute of limitations expired years ago. The victim died before the exoneration. There is no legal mechanism to hold the actual perpetrators responsible for the specific robbery that destroyed Windley's life — a structural gap in the justice system that compounds the injustice of every wrongful conviction.

03

The Reinvestigation

After Shanies Law presented the case to the Kings County District Attorney's Conviction Review Unit, the CRU undertook a thorough reinvestigation that confirmed what Kenneth Windley had been saying since the day of his arrest: he bought a money order, not a robbery.

The CRU determined the true identities of both suspects — whose nicknames and partial names Windley had provided to prosecutors before his trial, and whose full identities were never pursued. Both men were by then serving prison sentences for a pattern of seven robberies committed in the same Brooklyn neighborhoods, between April 2005 and February 2006, targeting elderly men returning from banks and check-cashing offices. The modus operandi was identical to the Ross robbery in every detail.

The two men confirmed Windley's account in recorded prison phone calls, written statements, and direct CRU interviews. They stated unequivocally that they robbed Gerald Ross and sold the money order to Windley, who had no involvement in the crime. The CRU reviewed corroborating evidence — including the appliance store records showing Windley arrived with two other men, one of whom handled the transaction and explained the money order to the manager — and found the account fully consistent.

The CRU concluded that had this evidence been before the jury, there was a reasonable probability of acquittal. Because retrying the case 20 years later was not feasible — the victim having died and memories having faded — the indictment was dismissed entirely.

Key Evidence Supporting Exoneration
1

Sworn statements from both actual perpetrators, confirming they robbed Gerald Ross and sold the money order to Windley

2

Recorded prison phone calls and email correspondence corroborating the perpetrators' confessions

3

Seven subsequent robbery convictions against the two men, all targeting elderly male victims leaving Brooklyn banks — same neighborhood, same method, beginning three days after the Ross robbery

4

Appliance store records showing Windley arrived with two other men who participated in the transaction and explained the money order to the manager

5

Windley's own consistent account — given to police before arrest, before trial, at trial, and throughout his 19 years of incarceration — confirmed in every material detail by the CRU investigation

6

Windley's use of his real name, driver's license, and home address at the point of sale — consistent with someone who had no knowledge of wrongdoing

The Work of Shanies Law

Wrongful conviction cases are among the most demanding work in the law. By the time a client reaches us, the official machinery has already concluded they are guilty — often multiple times, across multiple courts. The record is closed. The witnesses have scattered or died. And the person at the center of it all has been living with the consequences for years, sometimes decades.

In Kenneth Windley's case, the core facts were never in real dispute: he bought a money order from two men he knew from the neighborhood, used it to buy his mother a stove, and gave his real name and address when he did it. He told investigators this before his arrest. He testified to it at trial. He continued to say so for nineteen years. The system simply didn't believe him — or didn't look hard enough to find out whether it should.

Our work on this case centered on what the system had left unfinished. Windley had identified the two actual perpetrators from the beginning. He had partial names, nicknames, and enough information for an investigator to find them — which he eventually did, with the help of his fiancée and private investigators working on their own time. By the time we brought the case to the Kings County Conviction Review Unit, the exculpatory evidence was overwhelming. Our task was to present it in a form the CRU could act on, and to advocate for the outcome the evidence demanded.

On March 17, 2026, Kenneth Windley walked out of a Brooklyn courthouse with his mother and his fiancée. He said he wasn't bitter. "I'm just going to move on from there." That grace, after nineteen years, is his alone. The obligation to fight for it — and for everyone still waiting — belongs to us.
